01 — Problem

The problem no one names correctly.

Most conversations about motorcycle safety focus on the helmet itself — its material, its rating, its fit. Very few focus on the moment before the accident: the split second when a rider glances at a phone, skips a helmet because the trip is short, or doesn't realise their bike is moving streets away.

This project started from a different question. Not "how do we protect riders during a crash" — that's often a materials problem. But how do we reduce the situations that become crashes in the first place? And separately: when the worst happens, how do we ensure the response is immediate and automatic — not dependent on a rider who may be unconscious?

"The most dangerous moment isn't the crash. It's the five minutes before it, when someone decided they'd just quickly check that notification."
— Research note, contextual observation phase

There's also a theft problem that tends to get separated from the safety conversation, but in practice they're deeply connected. A stolen bike often means a rider without transport, sometimes in an unfamiliar location, at night. Security is a safety issue — so this project treats them as one system, not two features bolted together.

A full-stack IoT safety system — smart helmet, bike device, mobile app, admin panel, and server — is designed around one principle: the rider should never have to think about safety during a ride. It should happen for them: helmet enforcement at ignition, sync and theft signals, and emergency paths that fire without unlocking a phone.

02 — Research

What riders actually told me.

Before anything else, I needed to understand what riding felt like from an information-management perspective — not what safety experts say riders should do, but what they actually do, and why. I spent the first weeks in the field.

I spoke with nine riders across daily commuters, delivery riders, and occasional recreational use. Four kept a structured diary: every time they needed information while riding, or made a decision they weren't sure about. Entries ranged from "checked navigation at a light" to "heard a notification and didn't know if it was urgent." All participants gave informed consent; diary data was anonymised with no identifiers, and anyone could withdraw without consequence.

Three tensions that shaped every decision

Helmet compliance is situational, not habitual

Experienced riders wore helmets consistently — but for shorter urban trips, four out of nine admitted skipping it "sometimes." Reasons were practical (heat, distance), not ignorance of risk. Enforcement embedded in the bike's ignition changes the calculus: you don't negotiate helmet vs ride; they become inseparable.

Phone-checking is a reflex, not a decision

Riders didn't decide to check their phones — vibration happened and the hand moved. Any system relying on willpower or intentional choice was bound to fail. The interface had to work around the reflex: relevant information arrives without demanding eyes-on-screen in motion.

Emergency response depends on things outside the rider's control

When I asked what would happen in a serious accident, most paused. The honest answer: it depends who sees it. Location sharing was informal and manual. The idea that an automatic emergency alert with live location could fire with no action from the rider — even if unconscious — was met with something close to relief.

📌 Research implication: All three tensions pointed the same way — the system should require nothing from the rider during a ride. Ignition rules, emergency alerts, and theft detection should be automatic; the app is pre-ride and post-ride. During the ride, it disappears.

4 · Enforcement rules

Mandatory helmet rule

The bike will not start unless the helmet is worn and helmet and bike devices are paired and actively connected — hardware-enforced via ignition relay and server state, not a dismissible warning.

Device sync rule

If helmet and bike lose sync before or during a ride, the system triggers an alert — potential theft or unsafe separation without waiting for the rider to notice first.

What matters from an HCI angle: these rules shift enforcement from intention to architecture. You don't have to persuade someone to wear a helmet — the bike simply won't start without it. That removes a class of mode errors at startup.

Phase-by-phase detail

Phase A — Normal · Starting
Rider puts on helmet. Devices pair over BLE; ignition enables. App goes to background — no phone touch. Ideal state should feel invisible.
Phase B — Normal · In motion
GPS and connectivity monitored; abnormal events watched for. Silent unless a critical threshold is crossed — the "zero-touch during ride" commitment for the whole system.
Phase C — Normal · End
Ride ends when the bike stops and devices de-sync. Summary notification (distance, duration); data saved to history.
Phase D — Emergency
Crash detection or physical SOS: emergency contact gets an alert with live GPS. Works even if the phone is locked or the rider can't act.
Phase E — Security
Bike movement without authorised paired helmet → immediate rider alert; live location and remote ignition disable. Theft flow target: completable in under 30 seconds.
Phase F — Edge
Dead battery or failed device: temporary bypass via app or call center, admin verifies identity, time-limited (e.g. 4h max) with auto-expiry — legitimate emergencies without a permanent circumvention path.
💡 Phase F iteration: Early versions let riders self-approve bypasses; later ones allowed indefinite admin bypasses. Final design: automatic expiry and full audit logging — no permanent states, only bounded, accountable ones.
05 — Alert hierarchy

Not everything is urgent.

When every notification looks critical, nothing is. I separated informational updates from true alerts so riders aren't trained to ignore the channel.

Notifications (informational)
  • Ride completed with summary
  • Battery status update
  • Device connected or disconnected
  • Ignition enabled by admin
Alerts (critical)
  • Theft suspicion — bike moving without helmet
  • Helmet disconnected while bike is running
  • Emergency triggered by rider
  • Unexpected ignition force attempt

Notifications are dismissible. Alerts use distinct sound, persist on the lock screen, and stay in an in-app log until acknowledged — alerts never auto-dismiss.

Emergency contact copy was iterated the most: one screen-readable message with a browser-based live location link — no install on the contact's side, readable at 3am by someone who has never opened the product.

07 — Design principles

What held the system together.

Principles tested against every decision — especially zero interaction while riding. Several tie directly to HCI literature (multiple resources, mode errors, automation surprises).

01 📣

Notification-first

Critical information reaches the rider without opening the app during a ride; the app is for history and setup. Aligns with Wickens' multiple resource theory: concurrent visual tasks on the same channel compound attentional cost more than serial tasks.

02 🚫

Zero interaction during the ride

Any UI meant for use while moving is a distraction risk by definition. During the ride the app is a passive monitor — consistent with Norman's Gulf of Evaluation: if state is already clear, soliciting action adds overhead.

03 ⚡

Automation over instruction

Emergency and theft paths fire automatically; humans decide, they don't manually trigger under stress. Addresses mode errors (Sarter & Woods): manual initiation depends on accurate situational awareness you can't guarantee in a crisis.

04 ⏱️

No permanent states

Overrides and bypasses expire and are audited — no silent configuration drift. Echoes safety literature on automation surprises: indefinite state accumulation bites when users re-engage after inattention.

05 🔔

Signal, not noise

Informational vs critical channels use different weight. Alerts stay until acknowledged — they never auto-dismiss.

06 👥

Design for the 3am contact

Emergency contacts may see one message a year. Copy and format must work for someone who has never opened the product — browser-based location, minimal jargon.

09 — Key design decisions

Why the rules look like this.

Hardware-enforced helmet rule

The bike doesn't start until the helmet is worn and devices are paired — enforced by ignition relay and server state, not a dismissible warning. That moves safety out of the "I'll skip it today" loop.

💡 HCI angle: Shifting enforcement from intention to architecture removes a whole class of mode errors at startup.

Sync loss = alert

Losing helmet–bike sync before or during a ride flags theft or unsafe separation immediately — the rider shouldn't have to notice first.

Bypass: bounded, not permanent

Early concepts let riders self-approve or left bypasses open-ended — both undermined safety. Final model: admin-verified identity, maximum duration (e.g. four hours), automatic expiry, full logging.

10 — Evaluation

What testing showed — and what it couldn't.

Usability sessions with riders on the mobile app (SUS + NASA-TLX vs a phone-first baseline) and exploratory sessions with two fleet supervisors on the admin panel. Lab / simulated context — scores are directional, not a claim about real-road behaviour.

Central research question

Can a system that enforces hard safety constraints at the hardware level — constraints users didn't choose and can't override without friction — still reach acceptable usability without resistance, distrust, or workarounds? The hypothesis: if the constraint is invisible in normal operation, it won't feel coercive. Evaluation was set up to test that honestly.

Formal evaluation ran on two tracks: rider mobile flows and admin panel tasks. Rider sessions also used NASA-TLX against a baseline phone-based setup.

The two marginal scores — bypass and admin overrides — point at the same issue: exceptional states under stress. The happy path is smooth; edge cases remain the hardest design problem — which matches how most safety systems behave.

"I knew what it was doing. I just wasn't sure I trusted it yet. Give me a week of using it and I probably would."
— Participant 3, post-session interview

Trust in safety-critical systems isn't built in one session; it's built through predictable behaviour over time. That's the honest limit of lab evaluation — worth naming instead of hiding behind averages.

Honest reflection

Evaluation ran in simulation — not on real roads or commutes. Ethical and practical, but SUS and TLX are likely optimistic. Sustained attention and social workarounds don't show up in a controlled session. I believe the architecture holds, but I don't have field data for that claim.

I'd redesign the bypass flow from scratch: anxious user, bike won't start, hurry, plus identity checks — that deserves its own flow, not a stressed branch in settings. Designing the edge case as seriously as the happy path is something I underinvested in.

What I wouldn't change: the helmet rule as hardware constraint, not a dismissible warning — and designing four actors from day one. Admin, call center, and emergency contact shaped the rider experience in ways I couldn't have retrofitted.

Open research directions

Research question

Longitudinal field study

Does safety behaviour change when enforcement is architectural? A multi-week deployment with experience sampling could test whether the helmet rule holds socially and whether TLX gains persist on real rides.

Research question

Accessibility of emergency UX

Contacts with visual, cognitive, or motor needs; low-connectivity regions where a browser location link fails — underexplored with direct safety impact.

Design direction

Adaptive alert triage

Using context to weight urgency per rider raises a safety question: when does adaptive suppression become risk instead of relief?

Extension

Fleet scale

Moderate fleet sizes informed the admin IA. 40+ simultaneous riders could break a drill-down model — worth testing under high event density.
